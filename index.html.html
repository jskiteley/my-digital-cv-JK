01 — JAAQ
−9 days

Month-End Transformation

When I joined JAAQ, month-end close was taking 14 working days — nearly three full weeks. The root cause wasn’t complexity, it was unclear ownership. Nobody was certain whose job each step was, so tasks fell through the cracks and chasing became the default.

I mapped the entire close process end-to-end, assigned clear owners to every task, and replaced manual data pulls with automated feeds. The result was a standardised, documented close that the whole team could follow without relying on any single person’s institutional knowledge.

Within two months, close was down to 5 working days. More importantly, leadership started receiving management accounts faster — giving them the information they needed to make decisions at the pace a scale-up actually demands.

02 — hackajob
$25M raised

Series B Due Diligence

Fundraising puts every corner of a business’s finances under a microscope. At hackajob, I was involved throughout the entire Series B process — not just in a sprint at the end, but from the early preparation stages right through to close.

My focus was compliance and audit readiness: ensuring that every figure investors scrutinised was accurate, well-documented and defensible. That meant clean reconciliations, airtight audit trails and making sure nothing came back as a surprise during investor review.

The round closed at $25M, followed by a venture debt refinancing. Having a finance function that could hold up under sustained due diligence scrutiny was a meaningful part of getting there.

03 — hackajob
62→35 days

Debtor Days Reduction

62 debtors’ days is a significant drag on working capital — money owed but sitting uncollected for over two months. At a growth-stage business where cash runway matters, that’s not just a metric problem, it’s a real constraint.

I tackled it from both ends. On the invoicing side, I improved speed and accuracy so customers received correct invoices promptly, removing the most common reasons for delayed payment. On the collections side, I tightened the chasing process with clearer escalation paths and more consistent follow-up.

Within six months, debtors’ days had fallen from 62 to 35 — a 43% improvement that meaningfully strengthened cash conversion at a critical stage of hackajob’s growth.

04 — hackajob
3 countries

Multi-Entity Tool Rollout

As hackajob scaled across the UK, US and Romania, the finance stack hadn’t kept pace. Each entity was operating differently, with limited central visibility — the kind of setup that works at 20 people but breaks at 100.

I identified the gaps, researched the market, and put together a recommendation covering four tools: Payfit for payroll, Payhawk for expense management, Travelperk for travel, and Gusto for the US entity. I presented the business case to leadership and then led the implementation across all three countries.

The result was a consistent, scalable finance infrastructure — with central visibility over payroll, spend and travel that the business simply didn’t have before.

05 — JAAQ
Clearer reporting

Chart of Accounts Redesign

Leadership at JAAQ were struggling to get clear answers from the management accounts. The existing chart of accounts had become a barrier — categories were inconsistent, allocations were unclear, and it was hard to see what was actually driving performance.

I started by mapping the entire existing structure to understand what was working and what wasn’t. From there I rebuilt it from scratch, designing a new chart of accounts that reflected how the business actually operates — with clean department-level coding so P&L could be viewed by team for the first time.

The redesign transformed what management reporting could show. Leadership moved from vague top-line figures to a clear view of where money was being made and spent across every part of the business — exactly the visibility they needed to make better decisions.
